Transformation

The transformation of a formula to WNNF operates by ‚Äúpushing inside‚Äù negations. This is achieved by the function

wnnf : Formula ‚Üí Formula

which is defined as follows:

  • In the atomic cases the formula is unchanged:
wnnf ‚ä• = ‚ä•
wnnf ‚ä§ = ‚ä§
wnnf (` p) = ` p
  • In the case of binary connectives we just proceed recursively on the subformulas:
wnnf (œÜ ‚à® œà) = wnnf œÜ ‚à® wnnf œà
wnnf (œÜ ‚àß œà) = wnnf œÜ ‚àß wnnf œà
wnnf (œÜ ‚áí œà) = wnnf œÜ ‚áí wnnf œà
wnnf (œÜ ‚áî œà) = wnnf œÜ ‚áî wnnf œà
  • In the case of a negation, we push it inside. If it is in front of the constants ‚ä• and ‚ä• , then we just flip it to the other constant:
wnnf (¬¨ ‚ä•) = ‚ä§
wnnf (¬¨ ‚ä§) = ‚ä•
  • If it is in front of a propositional variable, then we leave it unchanged:
wnnf (¬¨ ` p) = ¬¨ ` p
  • Double negations are just removed (thanks to the law of double negation):
wnnf (¬¨ ¬¨ œÜ) = wnnf œÜ
  • If negation is in front of a binary connective, then we push it inside according to the corresponding de Morgan‚Äôs law:
wnnf (¬¨ (œÜ ‚à® œà)) = wnnf (¬¨ œÜ) ‚àß wnnf (¬¨ œà)
wnnf (¬¨ (œÜ ‚àß œà)) = wnnf (¬¨ œÜ) ‚à® wnnf (¬¨ œà)
wnnf (¬¨ (œÜ ‚áí œà)) = wnnf œÜ ‚àß wnnf (¬¨ œà)
wnnf (¬¨ (œÜ ‚áî œà)) = wnnf œÜ ‚áî wnnf (¬¨ œà)

For example, the WNNF of

œà‚Çá = ¬¨ ¬¨ (` p‚ÇÄ ‚áí ¬¨ (` p‚ÇÅ ‚àß ` p‚ÇÇ))

is

œà‚Çà = ` p‚ÇÄ ‚áí ¬¨ ` p‚ÇÅ ‚à® ¬¨ ` p‚ÇÇ

as we can automatically check.

_ = refl

Correctness

Now that we have a tentative definition of a function wnnf purportedly transforming a formula to an equivalent one in WNNF, we have to prove that this is the case. Soundness

The proof of soundness is conceptually simple and it is based on the double negation and de Morgan‚Äôs laws. The only difficulty is posed by the termination checker.

Simplification

The DNF structure allows us to simplify formulas to a stronger extend that what is possible with the generic procedure simplify . In this section we explore a simplification procedure which exploits the DNF structure.

We will implement three kinds of simplifications. The first two are based on the fact that a propositional variable should appear at most once in a clause, and the third one is based on comparing different clauses for subsumption:

  1. If a literal appears multiple times in a clause, then its repeated occurrences can be removed.
  2. If a literal appears positively and negatively in a clause, then the clause is unsatisfiable and can be removed.
  3. A clause can be removed if a logically ‚Äúweaker‚Äù clause is already in the DNF.
